a.

For a random effects model:

\(H_o : \sigma_\mu^2 = 0\)
\(H_o : \sigma_\mu^2 \neq 0\)

Decision rule:
We reject the null hypothesis if \(P(F_{calc} > F_{critical})\).

Assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\), and given that the probability of getting our \(\sigma_\mu^2\) under the assumption of the null hypothesis is close to 0 (P-value = <2e-6), we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the variability of sodium between brands beer brands is not zero \(\sigma_\mu^2 \neq 0\).

[1] 21.27375

\(E(\sigma^2) = MSE = 0.72\)
\(E(\sigma_\mu^2) = \frac{MS_{Tr} - MSE}{n} = 21.274\)

d.

[1] 0.9672634

Estimated \(\frac{\sigma_\mu^2}{\sigma^2+\sigma_\mu^2} = 0.967\)

This value means that approximately 96.7% of the variation in sodium content of lager beers in a metropolitan area is due to their brand.

b.

Testing:

\(H_o : \sigma_{\alpha\beta}^2 = 0\)
\(H_a : \sigma_{\alpha\beta}^2 \neq 0\)

Given the P-value for the interaction term (0.6823), and assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\), the probability is higher that our threshold. Therefore we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that we don’t have enough evidence to suggest that there is an interaction between player and location.

c.

Testing:

\(H_o : \alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 0\)
\(H_a : at~least~one~\alpha_i \neq 0\)

Given the P-value for the location term (0.8534), and assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\), the probability is higher that our threshold. Therefore we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that we don’t have enough evidence to suggest that location has a main effect on the mean points gotten by a player.

d.

Testing:

\(H_o : \sigma_{\beta}^2 = 0\)
\(H_a : \sigma_{\beta}^2 \neq 0\)

Given the P-value for the player effect (9.424e-12), and assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\), the probability is lower than our threshold. Therefore we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that we have enough evidence to suggest that the player has a main effect on the average points.
